# Inhaled steroids for bronchiectasis.

## Screening

- Decision: include
- Relevance score: 4
- Reason: Clear relevance to the review objective, evaluating inhaled corticosteroids for bronchiectasis.

## Detailed Summary

The study reviewed six adult randomized controlled trials comparing ICS with placebo or no medication. It found that high doses of ICS improved spirometry measures but not clinical outcomes like exacerbations or sputum volume in the short term, and had no significant effect on long-term outcomes.

## Relevance To Review

Highly relevant as it directly addresses the efficacy of ICS in managing bronchiectasis.

## Key Takeaways

- Inhaled corticosteroids may improve spirometry measures but not clinical outcomes like exacerbations or sputum volume in the short term for adults with stable bronchiectasis.
- Long-term studies show no significant effects of ICS on any outcome.

## Methods Summary

The review included studies from databases searched by the Cochrane Airways Group up to September 2007. It focused on randomized controlled trials comparing ICS with placebo or no medication in adults with bronchiectasis, excluding those with cystic fibrosis.

- Searches of Cochrane databases, MEDLINE, and EMBASE up to September 2007.
- Randomized controlled trials comparing ICS with placebo or no medication.

## Outcomes

- Spirometry measures (FEV1, FVC)
- Quality of life score
- Sputum volume
- Exacerbations
- Cough and wheeze

## Results Summary

ICS showed improvement in spirometry measures but not clinical outcomes like exacerbations or sputum volume in the short term; long-term studies found no significant effects of ICS on any outcome.

- High doses of ICS improved spirometry measures but not clinical outcomes like exacerbations or sputum volume in the short term.
- Long-term studies showed no significant effects of ICS on any outcome.

## Conclusion Summary

There is insufficient evidence to recommend routine use of inhaled steroids for adults with stable bronchiectasis, though they may be justified in certain subgroups. No recommendation can be made for their use during acute exacerbations or in children.

## Limitations And Missing Information

- Limited to adult studies; no pediatric data available.
- Only six adult studies met inclusion criteria.
